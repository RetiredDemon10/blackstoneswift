Here’s a clear overview of the main minerals and vitamins the human body needs, grouped by type and function.
Vitamins
Vitamins are organic nutrients needed in small amounts for growth, metabolism, immunity, and overall health.
Fat-Soluble Vitamins
These are stored in body fat and the liver.
Vitamin A
* Supports vision, skin, immunity, and growth
* Sources: carrots, liver, eggs, dairy
Vitamin D
* Helps absorb calcium and supports bones and immunity
* Sources: sunlight, fish, fortified milk
Vitamin E
* Acts as an antioxidant protecting cells
* Sources: nuts, seeds, vegetable oils
Vitamin K
* Important for blood clotting and bone health
* Sources: leafy green vegetables

Water-Soluble Vitamins
These are not stored much in the body and need regular intake.
Vitamin C
* Helps immunity, wound healing, and iron absorption
* Sources: oranges, guava, peppers
Vitamin B Complex
These help convert food into energy and support nerves and blood cells.
B1 — Thiamine
* Energy production and nerve function
* Sources: whole grains, pork
B2 — Riboflavin
* Energy metabolism and skin health
* Sources: milk, eggs
B3 — Niacin
* Digestion, skin, and nerves
* Sources: meat, fish, peanuts
B5 — Pantothenic Acid
* Hormone and energy production
* Sources: chicken, mushrooms
B6 — Pyridoxine
* Brain function and protein metabolism
* Sources: bananas, fish
B7 — Biotin
* Healthy hair, skin, and metabolism
* Sources: eggs, nuts
B9 — Folate (Folic Acid)
* DNA formation and pregnancy health
* Sources: beans, leafy greens
B12 — Cobalamin
* Nerve function and red blood cell formation
* Sources: meat, dairy, fish

Minerals
Minerals are inorganic nutrients needed for bones, muscles, nerves, hydration, and many body processes.
Major Minerals (Macrominerals)
Calcium
* Strong bones and teeth, muscle contraction
* Sources: milk, sardines, leafy greens
Phosphorus
* Bone health and energy production
* Sources: meat, dairy, beans
Magnesium
* Muscle, nerve, and heart function
* Sources: nuts, spinach
Sodium
* Fluid balance and nerve signaling
* Sources: salt, processed foods
Potassium
* Heart rhythm and muscle function
* Sources: bananas, potatoes
Chloride
* Fluid balance and stomach acid
* Sources: table salt
Sulfur
* Protein structure and detoxification
* Sources: meat, eggs, garlic

Trace Minerals (Needed in Smaller Amounts)
Iron
* Carries oxygen in blood
* Sources: red meat, beans, spinach
Zinc
* Immunity and wound healing
* Sources: meat, shellfish
Iodine
* Thyroid hormone production
* Sources: iodized salt, seafood
Selenium
* Antioxidant and thyroid support
* Sources: Brazil nuts, fish
Copper
* Red blood cells and nerve health
* Sources: nuts, liver
Manganese
* Bone formation and metabolism
* Sources: whole grains, nuts
Fluoride
* Protects teeth
* Sources: fluoridated water
Chromium
* Helps regulate blood sugar
* Sources: broccoli, whole grains
Molybdenum
* Enzyme function
* Sources: legumes, grains

Importance and Functions of Vitamins in the Human Body
Vitamins are essential nutrients that the body needs in small amounts to function properly. They help maintain growth, support body systems, prevent diseases, and keep the body healthy. Since the body cannot produce enough of most vitamins on its own, they must be obtained from food or supplements.

General Importance of Vitamins
Vitamins are important because they:
* Help convert food into energy
* Support growth and development
* Strengthen the immune system
* Maintain healthy skin, eyes, bones, and organs
* Help repair body tissues
* Support brain and nerve function
* Prevent deficiency diseases
Without enough vitamins, the body may develop weakness, infections, poor growth, or serious diseases.

Question 3 Digestion and Absorption
Digestion and absorption are important processes that help the body obtain nutrients from food. Digestion breaks food into simpler substances, while absorption moves the nutrients into the bloodstream for use by the body.

Meaning of Digestion
Digestion is the process by which large, complex food substances are broken down into small, soluble molecules that the body can absorb and use.
There are two types of digestion:
1. Mechanical Digestion
This involves physically breaking food into smaller pieces.
Examples:
* Chewing in the mouth
* Churning in the stomach
2. Chemical Digestion
This involves enzymes and digestive juices breaking food into simple nutrients.
Examples:
* Saliva breaking down starch
* Stomach acid digesting proteins

Process of Digestion
1. Mouth
Digestion begins in the mouth.
What Happens
* Teeth chew food into smaller pieces.
* The tongue mixes food with saliva.
* Saliva contains an enzyme called salivary amylase that begins digestion of starch into sugar.
Result
Food forms a soft mass called a bolus for swallowing.

2. Esophagus
The esophagus is the tube connecting the mouth to the stomach.
What Happens
* Food moves by rhythmic muscular movements called peristalsis.
Result
Food reaches the stomach.

3. Stomach
The stomach stores and mixes food with digestive juices.
What Happens
* Muscles churn food.
* Gastric juice contains:
    * Hydrochloric acid (kills germs and provides acidic medium)
    * Pepsin enzyme (digests proteins)
Result
Food becomes semi-liquid material called chyme.

4. Small Intestine
Most digestion and absorption occur here.
The small intestine has three parts:
* Duodenum
* Jejunum
* Ileum

Role of the Liver
The liver produces bile.
Function of Bile
* Breaks fats into tiny droplets (emulsification)
* Helps fat digestion
The bile is stored in the gallbladder before release.

Role of the Pancreas
The pancreas releases pancreatic juice containing enzymes.
Enzymes Digest:
* Carbohydrates → simple sugars
* Proteins → amino acids
* Fats → fatty acids and glycerol

Intestinal Enzymes
The small intestine also produces enzymes that complete digestion.
End Products of Digestion
Food Type	End Product
Carbohydrates	Glucose
Proteins	Amino acids
Fats	Fatty acids and glycerol
Absorption
Absorption is the movement of digested nutrients from the small intestine into the blood or lymph.

How Absorption Occurs
The inner lining of the small intestine contains tiny finger-like structures called villi.

Functions of Villi
* Increase surface area for absorption
* Contain blood capillaries and lacteals
Nutrient Absorption
* Glucose and amino acids enter blood capillaries
* Fatty acids and glycerol enter lacteals (lymph vessels)
The absorbed nutrients are transported to body cells for:
* Energy
* Growth
* Repair
* Storage

Large Intestine
Undigested food enters the large intestine.
What Happens
* Water and mineral salts are absorbed
* Bacteria act on waste materials
* Feces are formed

Rectum and Anus
Rectum
Stores feces temporarily.
Anus
Expels feces from the body through egestion.

Importance of Digestion and Absorption
These processes are important because they:
* Provide nutrients for energy
* Support growth and tissue repair
* Maintain body functions
* Help remove waste materials

Summary Flow of Digestion
Food intake → Mouth → Esophagus → Stomach → Small intestine (digestion and absorption) → Large intestine → Rectum → Anus







Question 4 Gynaecological Disorders and Their Management
Gynaecological disorders are diseases or conditions that affect the female reproductive system, including the uterus, ovaries, fallopian tubes, cervix, vagina, and breasts.
These disorders may affect menstruation, fertility, pregnancy, sexual health, and general wellbeing.

Common Gynaecological Disorders
1. Menstrual Disorders
These are problems related to menstruation.
Types
* Painful menstruation (dysmenorrhea)
* Heavy menstrual bleeding (menorrhagia)
* Absence of menstruation (amenorrhea)
* Irregular menstruation
Causes
* Hormonal imbalance
* Stress
* Infection
* Fibroids
* Poor nutrition
Management
* Pain relief medicines
* Hormonal treatment
* Balanced diet and exercise
* Treating underlying disease
* Medical check-up

2. Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)
PID is an infection of the female reproductive organs.
Causes
* Sexually transmitted infections (STIs)
* Poor genital hygiene
* Unsafe abortion
Symptoms
* Lower abdominal pain
* Fever
* Abnormal vaginal discharge
* Pain during sex
Management
* Antibiotics
* Proper hygiene
* Early treatment of STIs
* Avoid unsafe sexual practices

3. Uterine Fibroids
Fibroids are non-cancerous growths in the uterus.

￼
￼
￼
Symptoms
* Heavy bleeding
* Pelvic pain
* Frequent urination
* Infertility in some cases
Management
* Observation if small
* Medicines to reduce symptoms
* Surgery (myomectomy or hysterectomy)
* Healthy lifestyle and regular medical review

4. Endometriosis
This occurs when tissue similar to the lining of the uterus grows outside the uterus.
Symptoms
* Severe menstrual pain
* Pain during intercourse
* Infertility
* Heavy periods
Management
* Pain-relieving drugs
* Hormonal therapy
* Surgery in severe cases
* Regular follow-up care

5. Ovarian Cysts
Fluid-filled sacs that develop on the ovaries.
Symptoms
* Pelvic pain
* Irregular menstruation
* Abdominal swelling
Management
* Observation for small cysts
* Hormonal medication
* Surgery if large or painful

6. Cervical Cancer
Cancer affecting the cervix.
Causes/Risk Factors
* Human papillomavirus (HPV) infection
* Early sexual activity
* Multiple sexual partners
* Smoking
Symptoms
* Abnormal vaginal bleeding
* Pelvic pain
* Foul-smelling discharge
Management
* Early screening (Pap smear)
* HPV vaccination
* Surgery
* Chemotherapy or radiotherapy

7. Vaginal Infections
Includes yeast infections and bacterial infections.
Symptoms
* Itching
* Vaginal discharge
* Bad odor
* Irritation
Management
* Antifungal or antibiotic drugs
* Good personal hygiene
* Wearing clean cotton underwear
* Avoiding harsh chemicals

8. Infertility
Failure to conceive after regular unprotected intercourse over time.
Causes
* Hormonal problems
* Blocked fallopian tubes
* Fibroids
* Male factors
* STIs
Management
* Medical evaluation
* Fertility drugs
* Surgery
* Assisted reproductive techniques
* Counseling and support

Prevention of Gynaecological Disorders
* Maintain proper personal hygiene
* Practice safe sex
* Go for regular medical check-ups
* Receive HPV vaccination
* Eat a balanced diet
* Exercise regularly
* Avoid smoking and alcohol abuse
* Seek early treatment for infections

Importance of Early Management
Early diagnosis and treatment help to:
* Prevent complications
* Reduce pain and discomfort
* Preserve fertility
* Improve quality of life
* Prevent life-threatening conditions
